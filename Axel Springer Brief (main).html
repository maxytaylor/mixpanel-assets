Section 01

Two systems, two paradigms

Adobe Analytics and Mixpanel were built in different decades to answer different questions. Every divergence in your comparison sheet traces back to one row in this table.

Aspect Adobe Analytics Mixpanel
Unit of collection Hit (server call). One hit can carry many events. Event. One row, one event, one timestamp.
Identity Visitor cookie waterfall, resolved at collection time. Device-level. device_id + user_id, merged into distinct_id at query time. Cross-device.
Sessions visit_num stamped at collection. Frozen. Default 30-min inactivity. Computed at query time. Redefinable retroactively. Default 30-min.
Properties eVars and props with persistence rules set at implementation time. Borrowed at query time from any prior event in the user's history.
Consent Data feed includes all hits regardless of downstream consent. Filtering happens on read. SDK-level. Un-consented users never reach Mixpanel servers.
Conversion Within-visit by default. Cross-visit needs explicit eVar engineering. Cross-session natively. Conversion window is a query-time knob.
Section 02

The five drivers of divergence

Once you know which driver applies, every delta in the 28 April sheet has a clear explanation. Rate metrics diverge most because they compound numerator and denominator differences simultaneously.

Driver 01

Identity — the biggest one

Adobe resolves a visitor at the moment of hit collection using a cookie waterfall. On iOS, Apple's ITP aggressively resets these cookies — the same real user becomes a new visitor every time.

Mixpanel separates the two questions Adobe collapses: an anonymous device_id and an authenticated user_id. When both appear in the user's history, Mixpanel retroactively merges the anonymous history into the authenticated profile.

28 Apr: Adobe iOS DAU 1.1M vs Mixpanel 307k — but iOS article views are only 12% apart (2.5M vs 2.2M). The user counting methodology explains the gap, not actual user volume.
Driver 02

Consent — a structural floor

The PUR (pay-or-consent) model means Mixpanel never sees un-consented users. The SDK doesn't fire for them. Adobe's data feed includes all hits regardless of consent state.

This sets a permanent floor: Mixpanel user counts will always be lower than Adobe by at least the PUR opt-out percentage. It isn't a tracking failure — it's compliance working as intended.

Action: ask your DPO for the PUR opt-out rate for 28 Apr. That one number closes the iOS DAU conversation.
Driver 03

Hits vs events

One Adobe hit can carry up to ~100 events packed into post_event_list. Mixpanel is always one JSON object, one event, one row.

When both tools are measuring the same single-purpose event, you land within ±10-20%. Article PV proves it — 1.85M vs 1.9M on Android, 8% apart. Divergence appears when definitions disagree.

Watch-Through Rate showing 68% in Mixpanel is article completion rate (mp_article_closed ÷ mp_article_view), not video completion. Different metric entirely.
Driver 04

Sessions — same default, different math

Both tools default to 30-minute inactivity. But Adobe's session is frozen at collection time. Mixpanel's session is computed fresh on every query.

The critical finding: mp_app_open fires 1.85 times per session on average. AS have been comparing mp_app_open total events to Adobe Visits. Switching to $session_start brings Android within 4% of Adobe (1,056k vs 1,100k).

The correct Mixpanel equivalent of Adobe Visits is $session_start, not mp_app_open total events.
Driver 05

The denominator problem — why rate metrics diverge most

Rate metrics divide one count by another. Both counts can be computed differently in each system. Divergence on the numerator multiplies divergence on the denominator. That's why you see 2–5x deltas on rate metrics but ±10-20% on volume metrics.

Paywall View Rate (3% Adobe Android vs 7% Mixpanel): Adobe divides by Visits; Mixpanel divides by unique users. Switching the Mixpanel denominator to $session_start gives 2.8% on Android — within 0.2% of Adobe. The denominator was the entire difference.

Adobe formula
paywall hits ÷ visits
Mixpanel formula
unique(paywall_viewed) ÷ unique(app_open)
→ Switch to: unique(paywall_viewed) ÷ $session_start

Internal inconsistency in Adobe — worth flagging

Adobe shows Android DAU = 290k but Android App Open step 1 in the purchase funnel = 1,126k for the same day. These should be approximately equal — a unique user opening the app is by definition an active user. The 4x gap is internal inconsistency in Adobe, not a Mixpanel issue. Mixpanel's equivalent figures match exactly: DAU 419k = funnel step 1 419k.

Section 04

Reconciliation playbook

Six actions that, in our experience, resolve 80%+ of Adobe vs Mixpanel comparison concern. They don't require closing every gap — they require knowing why each gap exists.

1

Set variance bands per metric class — in writing

A metric outside its expected band is a signal to investigate. A metric inside its band is fine, even if "fine" is a 30% delta. Don't compare everything against an implicit "should match exactly" standard.

Single-event volumes   →  ±10–20%
User counts             →  ±20–40%
Session counts         →  ±30–50%
Rate metrics           →  align definitions first, then ±20%
Funnel rates            →  Mixpanel structurally higher (ID merge)

2

Align metric definitions before comparing

For each metric in the sheet, write down the exact formula in each tool. At least half of the 28 April deltas resolve at this step, before touching any data. The watch-through rate row is the clearest example — Adobe measures video completion, Mixpanel was measuring article completion. Same label, different metric.

3

Wire authenticated identity

Confirm which Adobe field carries the authenticated customer identifier (likely a hashed ID in post_cust_visid or an eVar), and ensure the same identifier flows into Mixpanel's user_id from the live SDK. When the IDs match, Mixpanel's ID merge retroactively stitches anonymous browsing to the authenticated user. This is the single most impactful technical action on the list.

4

Document the consent gap

Calculate the percentage of Adobe traffic that is un-consented for Mixpanel — users in the PUR opt-out path who appear in Adobe but never in Mixpanel. This is the structural floor for Mixpanel being lower on user-count metrics. Once documented, the iOS DAU question becomes a one-number answer rather than an open concern.

Ask your DPO to pull the PUR opt-out rate for 28 April from the consent management platform.

5

Treat funnel divergence as evidence, not error

If Mixpanel shows higher funnel conversion than Adobe, that's ID merge working as intended. The user who started anonymous and finished authenticated is one user in Mixpanel and two visitors in Adobe. Mixpanel's number is closer to what actually happened. Don't optimize this away — it's a structural advantage.

6

Pick 2–3 anchor metrics to deeply reconcile

Don't try to reconcile every row in the sheet. Pick the 2–3 metrics your business cares most about — for Bild, that's Article PV, App Open, and Subscription Started — and reconcile those exhaustively. Once the anchors agree, the methodology is established and the rest follow.

Section 05

The bigger picture

The success criteria for this PoC name four goals, in priority order. None of them are "exactly matches Adobe to the decimal point."

The PoC succeeds when a Bild PM opens Mixpanel on Monday morning, asks a question they've been blocked on for two weeks, and gets the answer in 30 seconds — not when DAU agrees to five significant figures with an Adobe report nobody trusted anyway.

Goal 01

Self-serve insight rate

PMs answer their own product questions without raising a ticket.

Goal 02

Time to insight

Minutes, not days. The answer is in Mixpanel before the meeting ends.

Goal 03

Cross-functional adoption

PMs, engineers, and editorial using the tool weekly — without training every session.

Goal 04

Perceived data trust

Confidence in the data and the event taxonomy. Not parity with a tool the team doesn't trust.
